Jump to Recipe Introduction

Oats are the perfect carb choice for a balanced breakfast. Not only are oats relatively quick to make, they are also packed with fiber and minerals, preventing a blood sugar spike, and leaving you feeling full.

The Ingredients

Rolled oats are soft and chewy. You'll typically see oats as steel-cut (Chopped groats - Chewy, coarse), rolled oats (Steamed & flattened - Soft, slightly chewy), or instant oats (Pre-cooked & finely rolled - Very soft). Rolled oats are the most versatile and can be used in baked goods or recipes like this one.

Saigon cinnamon is a specific type of cassia cinnamon. Saigon cinnamon is stronger, sweeter, and more aromatic than regular Ceylon cinnamon, but it contains more coumarin, so it’s best used in moderation if you eat cinnamon daily. Ceylon is milder and safer for frequent use. If you eat cinnamon frequently or in larger amounts (like daily in coffee, oatmeal, or baking), Ceylon cinnamon (regular cinnamon) is considered safer long-term.

Maple Syrup adds sweetness.

Salted butter adds flavor and texture, but you can leave it out and it won't affect the flavor too much. You can also get unsalted butter and just salt to taste.

Pink salt brings out the other flavors in the dish.

Water is to cook the oats in and create the porridge. You can substitute water for milk if you need more calories and fat.

Substitutes & Additives

Milk: You can use milk instead of water, but I prefer water because it tastes the same, though the water creates a more gelatinous oatmeal in my experience.

Brown sugar: If you don't have maple syrup, any sweetener will do. The next best option would be brown sugar, followed by white sugar, followed by other sweeteners like honey or agave sweetener.

Steel-cut oats: You can use steel-cut oats instead of rolled oats. Though steel-cut oats take longer to cook, they may offer you better benefits. Steel-cut oats are less processed (chopped groats) with a chewy, nutty texture, longer cook time (20-30 min), and lower glycemic index (42-53); while rolled oats (steamed & flattened) are softer, cook faster (5 min), and are better for baking like cookies, though both offer similar nutrition, with the main difference being texture and prep time, making steel-cut more satiating for breakfast and rolled better for baking.

Health Benefits

Oats support heart health, blood sugar control, digestion, and weight management, while also being a nutrient-dense, versatile whole grain.

Heart health: Oats are rich in soluble fiber (beta-glucan), which can lower LDL (“bad”) cholesterol. Oats also may help reduce blood pressure and improve overall cardiovascular risk.

Blood sugar control: The fiber in oats slows digestion, leading to more stable blood sugar levels, making them beneficial for diabetics and prediabetics when consumed in moderation and paired with protein and healthy fats.

Digestion: Beta-glucan and insoluble fiber in oats support healthy gut bacteria and help prevent constipation and promote regular bowel movements..

Weight management: Oatmeal's high fiber content increases satiety, reducing overall calorie intake and eating oatmeal for breakfast can help control hunger throughout the day.

Nutrients: Oats are high in B vitamins (thiamin, niacin, folate); minerals like magnesium, phosphorus, iron, and zinc; and contain antioxidants called avenanthramides, which may reduce inflammation.

Diets (Is it diet-friendly?)

Mediterranean - YES: Whole grains like oats are fully compatible. They can be paired with fruits, nuts, and olive oil for a classic Mediterranean-style breakfast. Focus on minimally processed oats and avoid instant flavored packs with added sugar.

Keto - NO: Avoid oatmeal on the keto diet. Keto diets focus on very low carbs (usually 20–50 g per day). Oats are high in carbs (~27 g per ½ cup dry), which can easily knock you out of ketosis. Use chia seed pudding, flax porridge, or coconut flour “oatmeal” for a low-carb breakfast.

Ketovore - NO: Even small amounts of oats are high in carbs (~27 g per ½ cup dry), which would kick you out of ketosis.

Carnivore - NO: The carnivore diet consists almost entirely of animal products: meat, fish, eggs, and some dairy. It excludes all plant foods, including grains, fruits, vegetables, and legumes.

Paleo - NO: Traditional oats are not considered paleo because the diet excludes grains, but you can enjoy grain-free "oats" made from ingredients like nuts, seeds, and coconut flakes, or have plain oats if you're following a more flexible version of the diet. Some modern paleo followers eat gluten-free oats occasionally, but traditional paleo would skip them.

Vegan - YES: Oats are plant-based, nutrient-dense, and a good source of protein when combined with nuts, seeds, or soy milk. Add fortified plant milk for calcium and vitamin B12 if needed.

Gluten-Free - MAYBE: Oats themselves are naturally gluten-free, but cross-contamination during processing is common. Make sure you use certified gluten-free oats.

Low-FODMAP - MAYBE: Small servings of oats (~½ cup dry) are usually okay for sensitive digestion, but larger portions may cause bloating in some people.

Diabetics - MAYBE: Oatmeal can be included in a diabetic diet, but there are some important nuances to consider. Oats have a medium glycemic index (steel-cut oats (42-53) have a lower GI than rolled oats (55-69)): steel-cut and rolled oats are better than instant oats, which spike blood sugar faster. Keep servings small to moderate (about ½ cup dry oats per meal). Pairing oats with protein (nuts, seeds, Greek yogurt) or healthy fats slows absorption and helps control blood sugar. Steel-cut or rolled oats are best for blood sugar control, whereas instant or flavored packets often have added sugar and digest quickly leading to a glucose level spike.

SAD - YES: Oatmeal is a healthy whole-grain source of fiber, vitamins (like B vitamins), and minerals (magnesium, iron); though if you aren't a very active adult, and/or you are somebody who needs to limit carbohydrates, it's best to limit oats.

Directions

Step 1:

In a small or medium sauce pan, add:

1 cup old fashioned rolled oats

2 cups water

0.5 tsp pink salt

Stir until well combined. Bring to a simmer over high heat, then reduce to medium-low and let it simmer until thick (about 10 minutes).

Note: The oats will bubble up and spill over the sides, make sure to keep an eye on the oats and stir to reduce the bubbling.

Step 2:

Once thick, to the same pot add:

2 TBSP salted butter

1 TBSP maple syrup or brown sugar

0.5 tsp saigon cinnamon

salt, to taste

Stir well, serve, and enjoy!
